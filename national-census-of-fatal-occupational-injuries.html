2007 National Census of Fatal Occupational Injuries

In 2007, there were a total of 5,488 work-related fatal occupation injuries in the United States, which was a minor decrease from the 5,703 fatal occupational injuries in the United States in 2006. The national rate of fatal occupational injuries per worker in 2007 was 3.7 per every 1000,000 workers, which was a slight decrease from the rate of 3.9 per 100,000 workers in 2006.

Notable findings in the 2007 Census on Fatal Occupation Injuries issued by the U.S. Department of Labor, Bureau of Labor Statistics include:

  • The overall fatal work injury rate for the U.S. in 2007 was lower than the rate for any year since the fatality census was first conducted in 1992.
  • Men account for a disproportionally larger rate of injuries in comparison to women, with fatal occupational injury rates per 100,000 workers of 6.4 and 0.6, respectively.
  • Workers aged sixty-five (65) years and older prove most susceptible to fatal occupational injuries with a rate of death of 9.9 per 100,000 workers in 2007.
  • The rate of fatal occupational injury occurrence steadily and consistently increased from youngest to oldest age demographics in 2007.
  • Fishers and fishing related workers claimed the highest rate of fatal occupational injuries with 111.8 deaths per 100,000 workers in 2007.
  • Finance and insurance workers claimed the lowest rate of fatal occupational injuries in 2007 with a rate of 0.6 fatal employment-related injuries per 100,000 workers.
  • Self-employed workers incurred 1,011 deaths due to fatal occupational injuries at a rate of 9.5 per 100,000 workers in 2007, which was down from 1,032 deaths stemming from fatal occupational injuries in 2006.

These statistics are initially preliminary, as the Department of Labor, Bureau of Labor Statistics issues revised editions of their annual findings later in the year.
